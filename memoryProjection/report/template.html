Global memory bit supply & demand

Quarterly, 2018Q1–2032Q4. DRAM and NAND modelled separately. Supply is bits the industry can physically make, through the tightest of six bottlenecks. Demand is bits the world would consume at constant 2025 real prices.

TL;DR

DRAM/HBM demand at 2025 prices runs ahead of supply past 2032. In the central case the gap widens from at end-2026 to by end-2029 — a deficit of about EB/yr — before flattening. The constraint that binds in the out-years is plain wafer capacity and EUV lithography. AI is the driver, and because an AI rack carries terabytes of ordinary DDR5 beside its HBM, the squeeze does not spare buyers who never touch an HBM stack.

Three things to know before you read any chart below:

  1. The gap is a pressure index, not a shortfall forecast. There is no price layer, so demand never self-corrects and the gap runs hotter than any real gap can. It measures how hard price and rationing have to work — not how many bits go missing.
  2. The model can barely produce a glut after 2027, by construction. Across Monte Carlo draws, the chance the gap ever closes peaks at . That near-zero is a fact about the model's shape, not evidence about the world — see what this model cannot show you. Everything here varies the size of the shortage, never really its sign.
  3. Everything through 2026Q2 is observed data, not model output. Supply is pinned to it and carries no error bar there. Demand is never observed — it is a counterfactual — so its band stays open all the way back to 2018.

The two lines — DRAM

Everything up to 2026Q2 is observed data, not model output — so the supply line has no uncertainty band there. After it, the shaded band is the P10–P90 range from a 400-draw Monte Carlo, widening with horizon.

The demand band never collapses, even over history. Supply was measured; "bits the world would consume at 2025 prices" is a counterfactual that was never observed and never can be. It is inferred in 2019 exactly as much as in 2031, and carries roughly ±15% throughout. In the top chart that band is thin over history simply because demand itself was small — it reads most clearly in the gap chart below, where the grey band over 2019 and 2023 is what "we do not know what buyers would have taken" actually looks like.

The GPU fleet — where AI memory demand actually comes from

Accelerator shipments are an output here, not an assumption: shipments = (new datacentre power)/kW + (power freed by retirements)/kW. The second term is replacement demand, and it is the piece an exogenous shipments series cannot see — retiring an A100 (80GB, 0.4kW) and refilling its watt with a Rubin Ultra (~1TB) is a large HBM purchase for zero net growth in power.

When is a GPU no longer worth its watt?

Two retirement rules, and the spread between them is the whole argument. A chip stays cash-viable for years after it stops being worth the watt it occupies — because when power is the binding constraint, the real cost of an old GPU isn't its electricity bill, it's the output foregone by not putting a frontier chip in that same watt.

Datacentre pipeline: announced vs actually built

Announced capacity and delivered capacity are very different numbers. Projects die in grid-interconnect queues, for want of transformers and turbines, on local opposition, or because the anchor tenant walks. Most of the shortfall is slip (it arrives late) rather than outright cancellation — the model tracks the two separately, because they have opposite implications for the out-years.

Which bottleneck binds, and when

Supply is min() over six candidate ceilings. This band shows which one is the gate in each quarter, and the line shows how much slack sits behind it — a constraint that binds by 1% is a different world from one that binds by 25%, because relieving the first just exposes the next.

But read it left-to-right for the more important result: no bar crosses zero. Move any single input by ±25% and the 2029Q4 gap still lands somewhere between and . Not one assumption in this model, pushed as hard as it can honestly be pushed, closes the gap. That is not a robustness win — it is a warning, and the next card explains why.

What this model cannot show you

The gap never closes above, and no scenario, band or slider closes it either. Before reading that as a finding, understand that the model is not able to produce the alternative.

Every historical glut in the backcast — 2019, 2023 — was made by two mechanisms, and both are switched off for the whole projection:

  • Producers cut output. The 2023 crash was a deliberate industry-wide utilisation cut to ~76%. Forward utilisation here is pinned at 96–97% — everyone runs flat out, forever. The model has no way to be disciplined.
  • Buyers destock. The 2019 and 2023 gluts were inventory drawdowns of −4.5 and −7.5 EB/yr. The inventory swing is held at zero from 2028. After the hoarding of 2026–27, the one thing that has ended every previous shortage is the one thing that cannot happen here.

So across draws, P(gap closed) never rises above in any out-year quarter. That near-zero is a property of the model's shape, not evidence about the world. The bands express uncertainty about how big the shortage is; they say almost nothing about whether there is one. Neither does the tornado.

Three further absences worth holding in mind. There is no price layer, so demand never self-corrects. There is no supply response — a 40% gap in 2029 finances no extra fab, though in reality it obviously would; memory is the textbook cobweb industry, and the roadmaps this model takes as given are precisely what a gap like this would move. And the whole demand line rests on the AI fleet reaching of IT load by 2032, roughly × today's — if that buildout does not happen, nothing else on this page matters.

The honest summary: this is what current roadmaps imply if nobody reacts. The reacting is the part that has been left out, and it is not a small part.
